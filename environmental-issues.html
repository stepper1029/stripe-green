Environmental Issues

Read below to learn about some of the world's pressing environmental issues. The first step to becoming an environmental activist is educating yourself on problems in the environment!

Climate Change

What is climate change?

Climate change is a change in the usual weather patterns of a certain area. When people use the term “climate change”, they are usually referring to the change in Earth’s climate due to carbon emissions. The Earth’s climate is always changing and it goes through cold and hot periods that can last millions of years. However, humans are accelerating the process significantly, to the point where it’s harming the environment.

What does climate change look like?

Climate change can look like many things, from an extra inch of rain to a hotter-than-usual summer day. Generally, it looks like dramatic and irregular weather patterns. Global warming is also a big part of climate change. In the last 100 years, the Earth’s average temperature has risen one degree Fahrenheit. It may not seem like much, but it’s enough to significantly damage ecosystems and the wildlife that live in them. Click here to read more about climate change and it's effects!

Source: NASA

Deforestation

What is deforestation?

Deforestation is the permanent removal of trees either for timber that can be used as fuel or to make room for agriculture, manufacturing, or construction. Beef, soy, palm oil, and wood products are responsible for most of the tropical deforestation. About 3.9 million square miles of forest have been lost since the beginning of the 20th century. In just the past 25 years, forests shrank by 502,000 square miles, and it’s estimated that a chunk of forest the size of a soccer field is lost every second.

Source: Live Science

Why do we need forests?

Forests are home to 80% of terrestrial biodiversity and in places like the tropical forests of New Guinea are home to 6% of the world’s species of plants and animals. Forested watersheds are responsible for 75% of the Earth’s freshwater. Over half the global population relies on these watersheds for drinking water and agriculture. On top of everything, forests capture carbon dioxide and emit oxygen. Tropical trees alone provide about 23% of the climate mitigation that’s needed to offset climate change. Not only does deforestation stop the mitigation of greenhouse grasses, but it also accounts for nearly 20% of greenhouse gas emissions. Forests are vital to the survival of the planet and deforestation is contributing to the ruin of our environment. Click here to read more about deforestation.

Pollution

What is pollution and where does it come from?

Pollution just refers to any substance that negatively impacts the environment. Pollution causes an imbalance in the ecosystem and contributes to global warming. Air, water, and soil pollution are the three main types of pollution that harm the environment. Greenhouse gasses, chemical runoff, and non-biodegradable products have a large negative impact on the environment.

What is the greenhouse effect?

Only 30% of the sun's radiation is reflected by clouds and ice while the other 70% is absorbed by oceans, land, and atmosphere. As the earth absorbs radiation and heats up, it releases heat in the form of thermal radiation that gets passed out into space. This is known as the greenhouse effect because the process of absorbing and releasing gas is similar to that f a greenhouse. Gases in the atmosphere that absorb radiation are referred to as "greenhouse gases" because they are the greatest contributor to the greenhouse effect. The greenhouse effect is on of the leading causes of global warming. The burning of fossil fuels and agricultural practices are the greatest producers of greenhouse gasses. To reduce your carbon emissions, try a plant-based diet or public transportation! Check out our advice on sustainability or read more about the greenhouse effect here.

Environmental Justice

What is environmental justice?

"Environmental justice is the fair treatment and meaningful involvement of all people regardless of race, color, national origin, or income, with respect to the development, implementation, and enforcement of environmental laws, regulations, and policies"(EPA ). The movement was primarily started by people of color who wanted to address the inequity of environmental justice in their communities. It is shown that lower-income communitities and people of color are disproportionally affected by the climate crisis. Look at our Additional Education tab to read more about environmental justice.
